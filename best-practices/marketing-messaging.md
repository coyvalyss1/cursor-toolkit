# Marketing & PR Best Practices

**Purpose:** Research-backed reference for messaging, positioning, and product-marketing pipeline. Use when writing copy (homepage, App Store, landing pages), planning launches, or aligning product and marketing.

**Source hierarchy:** We weight **tier-1 sources** (peer-reviewed or institutional research, proven publishers) over general blogs and agencies. When both exist, tier-1 is cited first; industry sources (Tier 2) are used for tactical how-to and recent benchmarks. See **Section 8** for the full list by tier.

## 1. Messaging by audience (tone, channel, copy)

*Tier 1: [HBR] intergenerational marketing; [Pew] social media & tech use by age. Tier 2: [1]–[4] — see Section 8.*

### Gen X (born ~1965–1980; ages 45–60)

- **Tone:** Direct, authentic, practical. No gimmicks, no condescension. Respect their time and intelligence. [HBR] argues marketing's future is intergenerational; Gen X is pragmatic and values authenticity. [1][2]
- **What works:** Real stories and customer proof over polished corporate speak. Word-of-mouth and reviews matter. Once they trust you, loyalty is high. [1][2]
- **What to avoid:** Over-the-top sales pitches, hype, treating them as tech-illiterate. They've seen every marketing fad. [2]
- **Copy:** Straightforward benefits; clarity over cleverness. Subtle wit/irony is fine; avoid flippant or Gen Z-style slang. [4]
- **Channels:** [Pew] reports ~74% of Gen X use Facebook, high YouTube adoption; internet use ~91%, smartphone ~90%. Hybrid digital + traditional (TV, radio, email). [Pew][1][3]
- **Value framing:** Quality and long-term value over flash. [NIQ] and [HBR] support focusing on long-term value and intergenerational relevance. [1]

### Millennials (born ~1981–1996)

- **Tone:** Authentic, values-driven. Brevity and informality in short-form (SMS, social). Punctuality matters. [4]
- **What works:** Social proof, purpose/impact, personalized content. Interactive and engaging formats. [4]
- **Channels:** Email, chat, social, mobile. Prefer tailored, personalized experiences. [4]

### Gen Z (born ~1997–2012)

- **Tone:** Visual, mobile-first, brief. Humor and relatability. Tech-savvy references, emojis, GIFs when it fits the brand. [4]
- **What works:** Value-packed, entertaining or thoughtful content. Instant, interactive, simple visuals. [4]
- **Channels:** Mobile notifications, chat, Instagram DMs, video. Don't assume they read long copy. [4]

### Baby Boomers (born ~1946–1964)

- **Tone:** Clear, respectful, reliable. Professional but with a personal connection. [4]
- **What works:** Straightforward, consistent information. Tradition and trust signals. [4]
- **Channels:** Phone, email, and traditional media still relevant. [4]

**Takeaway:** One-size-fits-all copy doesn't work. Match tone, length, and channel to the primary audience for each asset (e.g. homepage vs App Store vs paid social).

## 2. Marketing best practices (general)

*Tier 1: [HBR] product launch strategy, [McKinsey] GTM and product-led sales. Tier 2: [5]–[7].*

- **Positioning first:** One sharp sentence a 12-year-old could repeat. What we're launching, why it matters now, who it's for. Sell the outcome, not the implementation. [HBR] on product launches stresses market knowledge and clear strategy. [5]
- **Pre-launch:** Start marketing before launch. Validate with real conversations (not just surveys). Join communities, create useful free content, run small tests. [5]
- **Consistency:** Same pillars and value props across homepage, App Store, ads, and PR. [McKinsey] frames product marketing as critical to GTM; firms in the top growth quartile have 25–30% higher PMM-to-PM ratios. [6]
- **Trust:** Reviews, case studies, and third-party validation (PR, awards) matter more than brand claims. Gen X and Boomers especially rely on word-of-mouth and reviews. [1][2]

## 3. PR best practices (app / startup launch)

*Tier 1: [Forrester] B2C marketing/PR (trust, value, integrated strategy). Tier 2: [8]–[11] for tactical timelines and press kits.*

- **Timing:** Begin PR 3–4 months before release. Build media relationships 6–8 weeks before asking for coverage. Apps with early PR momentum are ~3x more likely to maintain long-term success. [8]
- **Narrative:** One clear, simple story. What, why now, for whom. [Forrester] emphasizes trust and value as core drivers; PR and social should be coordinated under one strategy. [8][10]
- **Targeting:** Right publications over big names. Tier-one tech (e.g. TechCrunch, Forbes, VentureBeat) for B2B/SaaS; niche and vertical for consumer/lifestyle. [10][11]
- **Press kit:** Fact sheet, key quotes, founder story, screenshots, links. Journalists are ~3x more likely to cover stories with complete press kits upfront. [8][9]
- **Benefits:** Third-party credibility, high-authority backlinks, leveling the field vs bigger competitors. [Forrester] ties PR to brand promise and customer-centric strategy. [10][11]

## 4. Product–marketing pipeline (connection and handoff)

Product marketing should connect **product, marketing, and revenue**—not sit as a one-way handoff.

### From strategy to execution

| Stage | Output | Who / what |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **TAM / SAM / SOM** | Who we serve, where we play, realistic share | Strategy / product |
| **Personas & jobs-to-be-done** | Pain points, motivations, language they use | Product + marketing |
| **Positioning & messaging** | One-liner, pillars, proof points | Product marketing |
| **Channel strategy** | Where we reach them (organic, paid, PR, community) | Marketing |
| **Campaigns & creative** | Ads, landing pages, emails, social | Marketing |
| **In-product signals** | Onboarding, tooltips, upgrade prompts, lifecycle emails | Product + marketing |

### Practices that make the pipeline work

*Tier 1: [McKinsey] product-led sales, PMM impact, usage-driven conversion. Tier 2: [6], [12], [13].*

- **Behavioral data:** Use product usage (sessions, feature adoption, milestones) to segment and trigger campaigns. [McKinsey]'s product-led sales model stresses digital demand gen, seamless product access, and data-driven personalization from usage. [12]
- **Single source of truth:** One doc or wiki for positioning, pillars, and approved copy. Homepage, App Store, and ads all reference it. [6]
- **Feedback loop:** Support and sales insights (objections, "why did you buy") feed back into positioning and messaging. Win/loss and interview notes → messaging updates. [13]
- **Revenue focus:** [McKinsey] finds companies with documented GTM strategies are 33% more likely to hit revenue targets; PMMs in top-growth firms have 25–30% higher ratio vs product managers. [6]

## 5. Quick reference: Gen X checklist for copy

- [ ] Direct and clear; no hype or gimmicks
- [ ] Real outcomes and benefits, not vague "transform your life"
- [ ] Respect time (concise; scannable)
- [ ] Trust signals (reviews, proof, "who's behind this")
- [ ] Tone: confident but not condescending
- [ ] No Gen Z slang or excessive informality
- [ ] One primary CTA per section

## 6. Software and app adoption: Gen X and Baby Boomers

*Tier 1: [Pew] social media and tech use by age; [AARP] 50+ technology trends. Tier 2: [14]–[19] — see Section 8.*

### Gen X (45–60)

**Social and daily use**

- **[Pew]** (Demographics of Social Media Users): ~74% of Gen X use Facebook; YouTube is the most widely used platform among U.S. adults (83% overall); Instagram ~47%. Gen X internet use ~91%, smartphone ~90%. [Pew][14]
- **LinkedIn** ~25% regular use (40–49); **Pinterest** ~40%. Increasing use of **Reddit, Threads, Substack**; **WhatsApp** and **LinkedIn** for support. [14]
- **TikTok:** [Pew] reports TikTok at ~33% of U.S. adults (2024); Gen X consumption often over creation. [14]

**Productivity, finance, and shopping**

- **Digital banking:** High adoption of online and mobile banking; prefer **simple, intuitive** interfaces and **security** over flashy features. [18]
- **Payments:** Security and reliability over convenience — ~51% rank security as top payment priority; ~49% primary debit, ~33% credit for big purchases; only ~5–6% use mobile wallets for travel/in-store. [18]
- **Shopping:** Mix of online and in-store; strong brand loyalty; willing to pay more for trusted brands. [1][2]

**AI and new tech**

- **AI tools:** Lower adoption than younger cohorts — e.g. ~36% of Gen X have used standalone AI (e.g. ChatGPT) vs ~76% Gen Z; ~18% use AI in their day-to-day job vs ~30% Millennials. **Awareness is high** (~78% know about ChatGPT). [19]
- **Barriers:** Privacy, data use, usability, and confidence in digital skills still matter. [AARP]

**Takeaway:** Gen X will try AI when it's clearly useful and low-friction. Emphasize clarity, security, and outcomes; avoid "AI for AI's sake" or overly casual tone. Facebook and YouTube are strong channels; email and clear landing pages work.

### Baby Boomers (58–78)

**Devices and usage**

- **[Pew]** (Older Adults & Tech): Smartphone adoption among Boomers rose from ~25% (2011) to ~68% (2019); more recent [AARP] data shows 50+ smartphone ownership ~90% and adults 50+ using an **average of ~10 apps** in a three-month period. [Pew][AARP]
- **eMarketer** reports "Most Used Apps on Mobile Phones Among US Baby Boomer Internet Users" (April 2024); full rankings typically require subscription. [15]

**Categories they use**

- **Streaming:** Netflix, music streaming. **Social:** Facebook, video calling. **9 in 10** (50+) use social media. [AARP]
- **Daily life:** Online shopping, **digital banking**, health record apps, **texting** (leading communication method). [AARP]
- **Concerns:** [AARP] and [Pew] note privacy, data use, usability, and confidence remain barriers for many. [AARP][Pew]

**Takeaway:** Boomers are on mobile and social (especially Facebook) and use a focused set of apps for communication, banking, health, and entertainment. Simple onboarding, trust signals, and "who's behind this" matter. Avoid clutter and jargon.

## 7. Decision-Making Framework

### When Writing Marketing Copy, Ask:

1. **Is it clear?** (Can a 12-year-old understand the benefit?)
2. **Is it authentic?** (Real outcomes, not hype?)
3. **Is it respectful?** (No condescension, no jargon?)
4. **Is it consistent?** (Matches brand voice and other assets?)
5. **Is it trustworthy?** (Proof, not just claims?)
6. **Is it age-appropriate?** (Matches target audience tone and channel?)
7. **Is it actionable?** (Clear next step?)
